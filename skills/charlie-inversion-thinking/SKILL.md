# Charlie Munger - Inversion Thinking

Munger's inversion framework teaches that the path to success runs through aggressively avoiding failure. Instead of asking "how do I succeed?" ask "what would guarantee failure, and how do I avoid those things?"

**Routes when user asks about:** inversion thinking, avoiding mistakes, pre-mortem, cognitive biases, checklist thinking, how to avoid stupidity, reverse engineering success, failure analysis, decision-making errors

---

## Phase 1: Context Gathering

Before applying any framework, understand the situation:

1. Ask the user: "What decision, plan, or goal are you currently working on that you'd like me to help you pressure-test through inversion?"
2. Read any relevant context the user provides — business description, current strategy, upcoming launch, investment thesis, or organizational change.

---

## Phase 2: Diagnostic Questions

Ask these questions ONE AT A TIME. Wait for each answer before asking the next. Adapt based on answers — skip questions that have already been answered.

1. "Walk me through the plan as it stands today — what does success look like, and what's the timeline?"
2. "If this fails spectacularly 12 months from now, what's the most likely reason? What's your gut telling you?"
3. "Who are the key people involved, and are their incentives aligned with the outcome you want?"
4. "What's the one assumption in this plan that, if wrong, would make everything else fall apart?"
5. "Have you gotten honest pushback from anyone — someone who told you this might not work? What did they say?"

Maximum 5 questions. Stop early if you have enough to work with.

---

## Phase 3: Analysis

Apply Munger's frameworks to the user's specific situation:

### The Inversion Protocol

Apply this step by step to the user's stated goal:

**Step 1 — State the Goal Clearly:** Restate what the user told you success looks like. Be specific.

**Step 2 — Invert (List Everything That Would Guarantee Failure):** Brainstorm aggressively. Ask: "If I wanted this to fail spectacularly, what would I do?" Generate at least 8-10 failure modes. Categories to cover:
- People failures: wrong team, misaligned incentives, key person left
- Market failures: wrong customer, too small a market, bad timing
- Execution failures: too slow, too expensive, quality problems
- External failures: regulatory change, new competitor, economic shock
- Resource failures: ran out of cash, couldn't hire, supply chain issue

**Step 3 — Identify Which Failure Modes Are Present:** Ruthlessly audit the user's current plan against the failure list. Check every item.

**Step 4 — Eliminate or Mitigate:** For each failure mode present, assess:
- Can it be eliminated entirely?
- Can its probability be reduced?
- Can its impact be reduced if it occurs?

### Pre-Mortem Analysis

Run a structured pre-mortem on the user's plan:

1. State: "Imagine it is 12 months from now. This has failed completely. What happened?"
2. Identify the top 5 failure causes, ranked by probability x impact
3. For each, specify whether it is a people, market, execution, external, or resource failure
4. Recommend specific actions to address the top 3 before proceeding

### The 25 Cognitive Biases Audit

Scan the user's situation for active biases from Munger's 1995 Harvard speech "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment." Check which of these are currently influencing the decision:

1. **Reward and Punishment Superresponse** — "Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome." Whose incentives are misaligned with good outcomes?
2. **Liking/Loving Tendency** — Is the user evaluating an idea more favorably because of who proposed it?
3. **Disliking/Hating Tendency** — Is the user rejecting a good idea because of who proposed it?
4. **Doubt-Avoidance Tendency** — Is the user deciding fast to relieve discomfort rather than to be right?
5. **Inconsistency-Avoidance Tendency** — Is the user defending a position because it's correct, or because they said it publicly?
6. **Curiosity Tendency** — (Positive) Is it being cultivated?
7. **Kantian Fairness Tendency** — Could a counterparty torpedo a deal because they feel treated unfairly?
8. **Envy/Jealousy Tendency** — Is envy distorting any evaluation?
9. **Reciprocation Tendency** — Are obligations from favors/gifts influencing the decision?
10. **Influence-from-Mere-Association** — Is the user evaluating something based on who referred it?
11. **Simple Pain-Avoiding Psychological Denial** — What uncomfortable fact is being avoided?
12. **Excessive Self-Regard Tendency** — Is the user overestimating their own competence here?
13. **Overoptimism Tendency** — What does the base rate say, not the user's optimism?
14. **Deprival Superreaction (Loss Aversion)** — Is a bad decision being made to avoid a loss that should rationally be accepted?
15. **Social-Proof Tendency** — Is the user doing this because it's right, or because everyone else is?
16. **Contrast-Misreaction Tendency** — Does this look good only because of what it's being compared to?
17. **Stress-Influence Tendency** — Is the decision being made under acute stress?
18. **Availability-Misweighing Tendency** — Is the most recent data point being overweighted?
19. **Use-It-or-Lose-It Tendency** — Are relevant skills being maintained?
20. **Drug-Misinfluence Tendency** — Is judgment impaired?
21. **Senescence-Misinfluence Tendency** — Cognitive decline consideration.
22. **Authority-Misinfluence Tendency** — Is the user accepting something because the person is impressive, not because the reasoning is sound?
23. **Twaddle Tendency** — Is discussion producing insight or just noise?
24. **Reason-Respecting Tendency** — Are the reasons being given actually good ones, or just any reasons?
25. **Lollapalooza Tendency** — Are multiple biases operating simultaneously in the same direction? If 3+ biases are pushing toward the same choice, this is the most dangerous cognitive situation. Stop and get an independent outside view.

### The Checklist System

Build a decision checklist specific to the user's situation using Munger's minimum decision checklist as the foundation:

- [ ] Is this within our circle of competence? Can we answer 5 basic questions about this domain?
- [ ] What would cause this to fail catastrophically? (Inversion)
- [ ] Which of the 25 biases are currently active?
- [ ] What does the base rate say? (Avoid overoptimism)
- [ ] Who has an incentive to mislead us here?
- [ ] What uncomfortable truth are we avoiding?
- [ ] Have we gotten an honest outside opinion from someone who will tell us no?
- [ ] Would we make this same decision if we couldn't tell anyone about it?

### Avoiding Stupidity vs. Seeking Brilliance

Apply Munger's hierarchy to the user's plan:
1. First: identify and eliminate avoidable stupidity (active biases, lack of preparation, acting outside circle of competence)
2. Second: assess whether the process is solid (checklists, outside views, inversion analysis)
3. Third: only then evaluate whether the plan has genuine brilliance

Apply the inaction test: "If you do nothing in this situation, is the default outcome acceptable?" If yes, doing nothing may be correct.

- [Bias 3 with specific evidence]

**Recommendations:**
1. [Most critical failure mode to address] — Why: [specific inversion reasoning]
2. [Second action — bias to counteract or assumption to test]
3. [Third action — process improvement or checkpoint to add]

**Pre-Mortem Top Risk:** [The single most likely cause of failure if nothing changes]

**Risk/Watch Items:**
- [What could go wrong that isn't in the current plan]
- [The uncomfortable truth the user may be avoiding]
