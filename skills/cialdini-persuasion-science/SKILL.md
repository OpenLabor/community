# Robert Cialdini - Persuasion Science

Apply Cialdini's 7 scientifically validated principles of influence whenever writing copy, designing pages, crafting emails, building pitches, or creating any asset where someone needs to say yes. Each principle exploits a genuine psychological tendency that evolved because it usually produces good decisions.

**Routes when user asks about:** persuasion, influence, why people say yes, sales psychology, marketing psychology, Cialdini, reciprocity, scarcity, social proof, authority, commitment, liking, unity, consent, ethical persuasion, conversion

---

## How to Use This Skill

When creating or reviewing any persuasive asset (landing page, email, pitch, sales script, ad copy), apply the 7 principles below as a checklist. Score the asset against each principle, identify gaps, and strengthen the weakest ones. Always apply Pre-Suasion — establish the right frame before the main message.

---

## Principle 1: Reciprocity

**The mechanism**: Humans feel obligated to return favors, gifts, and concessions. Gift → obligation to reciprocate. Concession → obligation to concede in return. Value given → goodwill and openness.

### How to Apply

**Content marketing as reciprocity**: Publish genuinely useful content — frameworks, templates, tools — before asking for anything. Free resources that require no email are more powerful reciprocity triggers than gated content (though gated content works for lead generation).

**The Free Sample**: Free trials, free consultations, free audits. The miniature gift creates felt obligation to purchase.

**First-Give Negotiation**: In negotiations, make a concession early. The other party will feel compelled to match, often with a larger concession.

**Surprise Gifts**: Unexpected gifts are more powerful than expected ones. Handwritten notes, unexpected upgrades, bonus content — stronger obligation than anticipated value.

### Checklist
- [ ] What value are we giving before asking for anything?
- [ ] Is the free value genuinely useful, or just a teaser?
- [ ] Is the first move ours? (whoever gives first has leverage)
- [ ] Is the gift personalized or personalization-adjacent? (personalized > generic)

## Principle 2: Scarcity

**The mechanism**: People assign more value to opportunities that are rare or decreasing in availability. Loss aversion amplifies this — we fear losing access more than we desire gaining it.

### Two Types of Scarcity

**Quantity scarcity**: "Only 12 remaining" — often works better because it implies social proof (others are buying).
**Time scarcity**: "Offer expires Friday" — hard deadlines with legitimate reasons outperform soft deadlines.

### How to Apply

**Limited editions**: Genuine limited editions create collector value and justify premium pricing. Works for physical products, digital cohorts/batches, and limited client slots.

**Waitlists**: Admitting demand exceeds supply paradoxically increases demand. Notion, Clubhouse, and Superhuman built buzz through waitlists.

**Early access pricing**: Combines scarcity (limited slots), reciprocity (they get a deal), and commitment (they bought early = invested).

### Ethics Checkpoint — Only Use Genuine Scarcity
- [ ] Genuine quantity limits (you actually have X units/slots)
- [ ] Real time limits (the cohort genuinely starts on this date)
- [ ] Honest capacity constraints (you genuinely can only serve N clients)

Never: "Only 3 left!" when you have unlimited inventory. Works once, destroys long-term credibility.

## Principle 3: Authority

**The mechanism**: People defer to credible experts. We respond to signals of authority even when underlying expertise is absent.

### Signals of Authority (ranked by strength)
1. Demonstrated expertise (specific results, methodology, track record)
2. Third-party credentials (publications, awards, media features)
3. Titles and affiliations
4. Professional appearance and presentation
5. Confident, specific language

### How to Apply

**Demonstrated > claimed expertise**: "We increased Acme Corp's conversion by 34% using this exact framework" > "We're conversion experts."

**Specificity signals expertise**: "Average client increases retention by 23% in 90 days" > "We improve retention." Vague claims signal low confidence; specific claims signal deep knowledge.

**Pre-Suasion**: Establish authority before the ask. Share a relevant credential, insight, or result before presenting the offer.

### Checklist
- [ ] Do we lead with credentials or results? (results are stronger)
- [ ] Are authority signals specific (numbers, names, publications) or generic ("industry leader")?
- [ ] Is expertise established before key conversion moments?
- [ ] Are authority claims third-party verified, not self-reported?

## Principle 4: Social Proof

**The mechanism**: When uncertain, people look to behavior of similar others. "If everyone is doing X, X is probably right."

### Social Proof Types (ranked by persuasive power)
1. **Peers/Similar Others**: "People like you are already doing X" — most powerful
2. **Celebrities/Influencers**: Medium power, declining trust
3. **Expert endorsement**: High power for credibility-sensitive decisions
4. **Crowd proof**: Raw numbers — "10,000 customers"
5. **Certification/Awards**: Third-party validation

### How to Apply

**Peer social proof**: "Founders at Series A companies use this" > "12,000 businesses use this." Specificity about who uses it beats raw numbers.

**Real-time proof**: "14 people viewing this page right now" — triggers bandwagon + scarcity simultaneously.

**Usage data as proof**: "Teams average 4.2 hours saved per week" shows adoption and results in one claim.

**Case studies**: Feature a customer the prospect identifies with. Not your biggest logo — your most similar customer.

**Warning — Negative social proof**: Never say "Most people don't do X" — it normalizes bad behavior.

### Strength Scoring (for each testimonial/proof element)

| Dimension | Weak (1) | Strong (5) |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| Similarity to prospect | Generic "business owner" | Specific "Series A SaaS founder" |
| Specificity of result | "Really helped us" | "Increased ARR by $2.3M in 6 months" |
| Credibility of source | Anonymous | Named person, company, photo |

Maximum score: 15. Below 9: rewrite or replace.

## Principle 5: Commitment and Consistency

**The mechanism**: Once people commit to a position, identity, or action, they act consistently with it. Small commitments lead to larger ones.

### How to Apply

**Foot-in-the-Door**: Get small yeses before large ones. Newsletter → lead magnet → webinar → free trial → paid plan.

**Public > Private commitments**: Asking someone to share their goal publicly increases follow-through.

**Written commitments**: Writing increases commitment strength. Onboarding flows that ask "What's your goal?" create commitment the user will feel compelled to fulfill.

**Active > Passive**: Active choices ("I choose A") create stronger commitment than passive defaults.

**The Identity Shift** — Most powerful form: tie commitment to identity:
- "You're the kind of person who..."
- "Our most successful customers are those who..."
- "You've shown you're serious about X by..."

Label customers with positive identities consistent with your product's benefits.

## Principle 6: Liking

**The mechanism**: We comply with and buy from people we like. Dramatically underweighted in business contexts.

### The 5 Liking Factors
1. **Physical attractiveness**: Use high-quality photos of real, attractive people
2. **Similarity**: "We're a team of founders who got frustrated by X" > generic corporate voice
3. **Familiarity**: Mere exposure effect — consistent brand presence
4. **Compliments**: Genuine compliments increase liking; hollow flattery doesn't
5. **Association**: Alignment with people/things the audience already likes

### How to Apply

**Brand voice**: Brands with distinct, likable personalities outperform generic ones. This is liking at scale.

**Founder-led marketing**: A real person with real opinions is more likable than a faceless brand. Authentic failures are more likable than polished successes.

**Mirror customer language**: Use the words they use, reference the problems they describe, reflect their worldview.

---

## Principle 7: Unity

**The mechanism**: We are influenced by those who are part of our "we" — tribe, family, community. Goes beyond liking to shared identity.

**Unity vs. Liking**: Liking = "You're similar to me." Unity = "You are one of us."

### How to Apply

**Community building**: Users who feel part of a community (not just a user base) have dramatically higher retention. The "we" identity makes leaving feel like abandoning the tribe.

**Shared struggle**: "We built this because we suffered through the same problem" creates unity. Shared struggle binds more strongly than shared success.

**Co-creation**: Involving customers in product decisions creates invested advocates. Advisory boards, beta groups, design partners.

**Insider language**: Communities develop their own vocabulary. Adopt and amplify it.

## The Pre-Suasion Framework

From *Pre-Suasion* (2016): the moment before the request is as important as the request itself. Priming the right mental state doubles effectiveness.

**Attention directs focus**: What people attend to first shapes how they process everything after. Start with the customer's most acute pain.

**Unity before ask**: Establish shared identity before making a request. "As someone who cares about X..."

**Credibility before content**: State most relevant credential in the first sentence.

**Positive framing**: "Are you an adventurous person?" before an adventurous ask — they'll act consistently with "yes."
